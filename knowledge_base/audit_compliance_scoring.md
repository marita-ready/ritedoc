# NDIS Audit Compliance Scoring

This document outlines the NDIS Commission's audit methodology, the conformity rating scale, and the specific evidence requirements auditors look for when reviewing progress notes and documentation. It forms the basis for RiteDoc's internal 5-pillar scoring system.

## 1. NDIS Commission Audit Methodology

The NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission uses a structured audit process to assess registered providers against the NDIS Practice Standards. The type of audit depends on the risk and complexity of the supports provided.

### 1.1 Types of Audits

*   **Verification Audit:** A desktop review of documentary evidence for providers delivering lower-risk, lower-complexity supports. It verifies that the provider has the necessary policies, procedures, and qualifications in place.
*   **Certification Audit:** A more rigorous, two-stage process for providers delivering higher-risk or more complex supports (e.g., High Intensity Daily Personal Activities, Specialist Behaviour Support, Early Childhood Supports, Specialist Disability Accommodation).
    *   **Stage 1 (Desktop Audit):** An off-site review of the provider's documented policies, procedures, and systems to ensure they align with the NDIS Practice Standards.
    *   **Stage 2 (Onsite Audit):** An on-site assessment to verify that the documented systems are being implemented effectively in practice. This involves reviewing records (including progress notes), interviewing staff and participants, and observing service delivery.
*   **Mid-Term Audit:** Conducted approximately 18 months into a provider's registration period (for those who underwent a certification audit) to ensure ongoing compliance, particularly focusing on governance and operational management.
*   **Condition Audit:** An audit required by the NDIS Commission at any time during the registration period to address specific concerns or monitor compliance with registration conditions.
*   **Out-of-Cycle Audit:** Required when a provider wishes to add new, higher-risk registration groups to their scope of services.

### 1.2 The 4-Point Conformity Rating Scale

Auditors assess a provider's performance against each relevant Quality Indicator within the NDIS Practice Standards using a standardized 4-point rating scale.

| Rating | Description | Implication for Certification |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **3** | **Conforms with elements of best practice:** The provider not only meets the requirements of the NDIS Practice Standards but demonstrates innovative, highly effective, or sector-leading practices that exceed the minimum expectations. | Strongly supports certification. |
| **2** | **Conforms with NDIS Practice Standards:** The provider demonstrates that their systems, processes, and practices meet the requirements of the relevant Quality Indicators. Evidence is consistent and reliable. | Supports certification. |
| **1** | **Minor Non-Conformity:** The provider's systems or practices partially meet the requirements, but there is a minor gap or inconsistency. This does not pose an immediate risk to participants but requires a corrective action plan. | Certification may be recommended subject to an approved corrective action plan. |
| **0** | **Major Non-Conformity:** The provider fails to meet the requirements of a Quality Indicator, and this failure poses a significant risk to the safety, health, or wellbeing of participants, or indicates a systemic failure in the provider's governance or service delivery. | **Precludes a recommendation for certification.** The provider must resolve the major non-conformity before certification can be granted. |

## 2. Evidence Requirements: What Auditors Look For

During a Stage 2 Certification Audit, auditors rely heavily on documented evidence, particularly progress notes, to verify that supports are being delivered safely, effectively, and in accordance with the participant's NDIS plan and the provider's policies.

### 2.1 The "Golden Thread" of Evidence

Auditors look for a "golden thread" that connects the participant's NDIS plan goals to the specific supports delivered and the outcomes achieved. This thread must be visible in the progress notes.

1.  **NDIS Plan Goal:** What is the participant trying to achieve?
2.  **Support Plan/Service Agreement:** How will the provider support the participant to achieve this goal?
3.  **Progress Note (The Evidence):** What specific actions did the worker take during the shift to implement the support plan?
4.  **Outcome:** What was the result of the support provided? Did it move the participant closer to their goal?

### 2.2 Key Elements of a Compliant Progress Note

To score a '2' (Conforms) or '3' (Best Practice) on the conformity scale, progress notes must consistently demonstrate the following elements:

*   **Objective Observations:** Notes must be factual, specific, and objective. Vague statements like "had a good day" or "seemed happy" are insufficient. Auditors look for descriptions of observable behaviours, specific tasks completed, and the level of assistance required (e.g., "Participant independently prepared a sandwich using verbal prompts for sequencing").
*   **Clear Goal Alignment:** Every progress note must explicitly link the support provided to a specific goal in the participant's NDIS plan or the provider's support plan. This justifies the funding and demonstrates purposeful service delivery.
*   **Participant Voice and Choice:** Notes must capture the participant's perspective, preferences, and decisions. This demonstrates that supports are person-centred and that the participant is exercising choice and control. Direct quotes or descriptions of how the participant directed the support are highly valued.
*   **Risk and Practice Alert Implementation:** If a participant has specific health risks (e.g., dysphagia, epilepsy) or a Behaviour Support Plan, the progress notes must show that the relevant protocols or practice alerts were followed during the shift.
*   **Measurable Outcomes:** Notes should describe the specific outcome of the support provided, allowing for tracking of progress over time.

### 2.3 Common Non-Conformities in Documentation

Auditors frequently identify the following issues in progress notes, which can lead to minor or major non-conformities:

*   **Lack of Goal Linkage:** Notes describe activities (e.g., "went to the park") without explaining how they relate to the participant's goals.
*   **Subjective Language:** Over-reliance on the worker's interpretation of the participant's feelings rather than objective observations.
*   **Copy-Pasting/Repetitive Notes:** Notes that are identical or highly similar across multiple shifts, suggesting a lack of individualized support or poor record-keeping practices.
*   **Missing Safety Information:** Failure to document whether incidents occurred, or failing to record the administration of medication or adherence to health protocols.
*   **The "Evidence Gap":** A disconnect between the hours billed and the support documented. If a 4-hour shift is billed, the progress note must reasonably reflect 4 hours of purposeful support.

## 3. RiteDoc's 5-Pillar Scoring System

RiteDoc uses an internal 5-pillar scoring system to evaluate the compliance and quality of drafted progress notes before they are finalized. This system aligns with the NDIS Commission's audit methodology and evidence requirements.

The five pillars are:

1.  **Goal Linkage:** Assesses whether the note explicitly connects the support delivered to a specific NDIS plan goal.
2.  **Participant Voice:** Evaluates the extent to which the note captures the participant's choices, preferences, and perspective.
3.  **Measurable Outcomes:** Checks for specific, observable outcomes resulting from the support provided.
4.  **Support Delivered:** Assesses the clarity and detail of the description of the actual support activities undertaken.
5.  **Risk & Safety:** Verifies that safety concerns, incidents, or adherence to health protocols are appropriately documented or that a clear "nil-incident" statement is included.

Each pillar is scored internally from 0 to 3, mirroring the NDIS conformity scale. These scores drive the traffic light system (Green, Orange, Red) presented to the user, ensuring that only notes meeting the standard of a '2' (Conforms) or higher across all pillars are marked as audit-ready.
