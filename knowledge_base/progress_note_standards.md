# NDIS Progress Note Standards

This document establishes the definitive standards for NDIS progress notes, distinguishing them from case notes, defining the "24-Hour Rule," and outlining the specific requirements for different support types (SIL, Community Access, In-Home Support). It serves as the core reference for RiteDoc's AI rewriting engine.

## 1. Progress Notes vs. Case Notes

A fundamental distinction in NDIS documentation is the difference between a progress note and a case note. While both are essential for compliance, they serve different purposes and are written under different circumstances.

### 1.1 Progress Notes (Daily Operational Evidence)

Progress notes are the routine, daily records of support delivery. They are written after every shift or service instance. Their primary purpose is to provide operational evidence that the billed hours match the delivered support and that the service aligns with the participant's NDIS plan.

**Key Characteristics of Progress Notes:**
*   **Frequency:** Written after every shift or service.
*   **Focus:** Describes what activities occurred, how the participant engaged, observable changes, alignment with goals, and immediate wellbeing.
*   **Auditor Use:** Used to verify service delivery against billing and to track incremental progress toward goals.
*   **Content:** Factual, objective observations of the shift's events.

### 1.2 Case Notes (Strategic Evidence)

Case notes are written when a significant event, change, or decision occurs that falls outside the scope of routine daily support. They demonstrate that the provider is managing risk appropriately, responding to changes in the participant's needs, and exercising professional judgement.

**Key Characteristics of Case Notes:**
*   **Frequency:** Written only when a significant event occurs.
*   **Focus:** Documents changes in behaviour or health, risk incidents or near misses, updates to care plans, complex decision-making, or discussions with families/allied health professionals.
*   **Auditor Use:** Used to assess the provider's governance, risk management, and responsiveness to participant needs.
*   **Content:** Detailed accounts of incidents, clinical observations, strategic decisions, and follow-up actions.

## 2. The "24-Hour Rule" and the Evidence Gap

The "24-Hour Rule" is a critical compliance standard in NDIS documentation. It dictates that progress notes must be written and finalized within 24 hours of the shift ending.

### 2.1 Why the 24-Hour Rule Matters

*   **Accuracy:** Memory fades quickly. Notes written immediately after a shift are significantly more accurate and reliable than those written days later.
*   **Continuity of Care:** The next support worker on shift relies on the previous shift's notes to understand the participant's current status, any ongoing issues, or changes in routine. Delayed notes compromise continuity of care.
*   **Incident Reporting:** If an incident occurred during the shift, the 24-hour reporting window for critical incidents (e.g., death, serious injury, abuse) begins immediately. Delayed progress notes can lead to missed reporting deadlines and severe compliance breaches.

### 2.2 The Evidence Gap

The "Evidence Gap" refers to the discrepancy between the hours a provider bills the NDIS and the amount of support documented in the progress notes.

*   **Auditor Scrutiny:** Auditors actively look for the evidence gap. If a provider bills for a 6-hour shift, but the progress note only describes 30 minutes of activity (e.g., "Helped with lunch. All good."), the auditor will question what occurred during the remaining 5.5 hours.
*   **Funding Recovery:** A significant evidence gap can lead to findings of non-conformity and, in severe cases, the NDIA may recover funds for unsupported billing.
*   **Closing the Gap:** Progress notes must reasonably reflect the duration of the shift. While every minute doesn't need to be detailed, the note should provide a comprehensive overview of the support delivered throughout the billed period.

## 3. Documentation by Support Type

Different types of NDIS supports require specific details in their progress notes to demonstrate compliance and quality of care.

### 3.1 Supported Independent Living (SIL)

SIL supports are typically high-intensity and involve 24/7 care in a shared living environment. Documentation must reflect this complexity.

**Specific Requirements for SIL Notes:**
*   **Shift Handovers:** Notes must clearly document the handover of information between shifts, including any changes in the participant's health, behaviour, or routine.
*   **Health Protocols:** Strict adherence to any health management plans (e.g., complex bowel care, dysphagia management) must be explicitly documented.
*   **Behaviour Support:** If a Behaviour Support Plan is in place, notes must detail any proactive or reactive strategies used, and any use of restrictive practices must be recorded precisely.
*   **Household Dynamics:** Observations regarding the participant's interactions with housemates and their participation in shared household tasks.
*   **Overnight Support:** For active overnight shifts, notes must detail the support provided during the night. For inactive (sleepover) shifts, any instances where the worker was required to provide support must be documented.

### 3.2 Community Access (Social and Community Participation)

Community access supports focus on enabling the participant to engage in social, recreational, or civic activities outside their home.

**Specific Requirements for Community Access Notes:**
*   **Location and Activity:** Clearly state where the participant went and what activities they engaged in.
*   **Goal Alignment:** Explicitly link the community activity to a specific NDIS plan goal (e.g., "Goal 3: Increase social participation and build community connections").
*   **Level of Engagement:** Describe how the participant interacted with others and their environment. Did they actively participate, or were they a passive observer?
*   **Transport:** Document the mode of transport used and any support required during transit.
*   **Financial Transactions:** If the worker assisted the participant with purchases, this must be documented to safeguard the participant's money.

### 3.3 In-Home Support (Core Supports)

In-home supports typically involve assistance with daily living tasks, personal care, and household management.

**Specific Requirements for In-Home Support Notes:**
*   **Specific Tasks:** Detail the exact tasks the worker assisted with (e.g., showering, dressing, meal preparation, cleaning).
*   **Level of Assistance:** Describe the type of support provided for each task (e.g., physical assistance, verbal prompting, supervision, independent completion).
*   **Skill Building:** If the goal is to increase independence, document any progress the participant made in completing tasks with less assistance.
*   **Environment:** Note any observations regarding the safety and cleanliness of the home environment.

## 4. Common Documentation Red Flags

Auditors and compliance teams look for specific "red flags" in progress notes that indicate poor quality, potential risk, or non-compliance.

*   **Subjective Language:** Using words like "happy," "sad," "angry," or "good" without providing objective evidence to support the interpretation.
*   **Vague Descriptions:** Phrases like "provided support," "helped out," or "routine care" that do not explain what actually happened.
*   **Copy and Paste:** Identical notes submitted for multiple shifts, indicating that the worker is not documenting the specific events of each shift.
*   **Missing Signatures/Identifiers:** Notes that do not clearly identify the worker who provided the support and wrote the note.
*   **Late Entries:** Notes written significantly after the 24-hour window, compromising accuracy.
*   **Unexplained Incidents:** Mentioning an injury, behavioural escalation, or medication error without completing the corresponding incident report.
*   **Lack of Participant Voice:** Notes that only describe what the worker did, without mentioning the participant's choices, preferences, or reactions.
